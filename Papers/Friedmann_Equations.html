Abstract

We present a reformulation of the Friedmann equations under the hypothesis that cosmic evolution proceeds via discrete time increments—digital ticks—each of duration \( \tau = 0.6\,\text{ps} \). Within this framework, each tick acts as an error-correcting event that synchronizes the coherent energy field. Coupling this discrete ticking with an effective fractal spatial integration in \( D_{\text{eff}} = 2.667 \) dimensions, we recalibrate gravitational dynamics so that baryonic matter alone fully accounts for cosmic evolution. In this picture, the observed expansion emerges as a cumulative phase drift from tick-induced corrections, obviating the need for dark energy.

Introduction

The Friedmann equations are central to modern cosmology, describing how the scale factor \( a(t) \) evolves in a continuously expanding universe based on energy density, spatial curvature, and the cosmological constant. Standard models require extra components (such as dark energy) to match observations. Here, we propose a discrete framework where time is segmented into fundamental ticks and space is recalibrated by an effective fractal dimension. This approach naturally integrates quantum and gravitational dynamics, modifying the Friedmann equations so that cosmic evolution arises solely from the interactions of baryonic matter.

Discrete Time and Cosmic Evolution

In our model, time is quantized into fixed intervals: \[ \tau = 0.6\,\text{ps}. \] Defining the tick count \( N \) such that \[ t = N \tau, \] the continuous scale factor \( a(t) \) is replaced by its discrete analogue \( a_N \). The evolution of the scale factor is then described by the finite difference \[ a_{N+1} = a_N + \Delta a_N, \] where \( \Delta a_N \) represents the net effect of synchronization (or error-correction) at each tick.

Reformulated Friedmann Equation

The standard Friedmann equation is expressed as \[ \left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{k}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda}{3}. \] In the discrete framework, the time derivative is approximated by a finite difference: \[ \frac{\dot{a}}{a} \approx \frac{a_{N+1} - a_N}{\tau\, a_N}. \] Since our derivation fully accounts for baryonic matter (i.e. \( p_{\text{total}} = p_{\text{matter}} \)) and no excess vacuum energy exists, we set \[ \Lambda \approx 0. \] Moreover, the standard 3D volume element is modified to an effective integration over a fractal spatial domain: \[ dV_{\text{eff}} \propto r^{D_{\text{eff}}-1}\,dr = r^{1.667}\,dr \quad \text{with} \quad D_{\text{eff}} = 2.667. \] This recalibration yields an effective gravitational constant: \[ G_{\text{eff}} = G \left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{3-D_{\text{eff}}} = G \left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{0.333}, \] where \( r_0 \) is a normalization scale.

Therefore, the discrete Friedmann equation is reformulated as: \[ \left(\frac{a_{N+1} - a_N}{\tau\, a_N}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G_{\text{eff}}}{3}\rho_{\text{baryon}} - \frac{k_{\text{eff}}}{a_N^2}. \] Here, \( k_{\text{eff}} \) represents the curvature term adjusted for our discrete, fractal integration.

Tick-Induced Redshift

Traditionally, redshift is defined by: \[ 1 + z = \frac{a(t_0)}{a(t_{\text{emit}})}. \] In contrast, within our discrete framework, redshift arises from cumulative phase shifts induced by each tick. Assuming each tick introduces a small phase shift \( \delta \), after \( N \) ticks the total phase drift is given by: \[ 1 + z \approx \exp\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \delta_i\right), \] which, for small and nearly constant \( \delta \), simplifies to \[ z \approx N\,\delta. \] Thus, the observed redshift reflects the history of tick-induced error corrections rather than a continuous stretching of space.

Discussion

The modified Friedmann equation demonstrates that cosmic dynamics—including gravitational interactions and redshift phenomena—can be entirely derived from the interplay of discretized ticks and baryonic matter, recalibrated through an effective fractal spatial dimension. This framework eliminates the need for extraneous components, like dark energy, by showing that all energy contributions are inherently maintained through periodic error corrections. Redshift, rather than being a measure of continuous expansion, instead records the cumulative phase adjustments over cosmic time.

Conclusion

We have derived a modified formulation of the Friedmann equations by discretizing time into fundamental intervals of \( \tau = 0.6\,\text{ps} \) and recalibrating spatial integration to an effective dimension \( D_{\text{eff}} = 2.667 \). This new approach unifies cosmic evolution with quantum-scale synchronization events, explaining both gravitational dynamics and redshift solely in terms of baryonic matter undergoing discrete error correction. Future research will focus on testing the predictions of this discrete ticking model against observational data.
