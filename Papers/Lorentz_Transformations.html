Lorentz Transformations: A Digital Tick Approach

Abstract

We introduce a novel framework in which the invariance of the speed of light emerges naturally from an invariant digital tick—a fundamental, unchanging metronome that underpins spacetime. Rather than relying on the intricate coordinate-dependent time dilation and length contraction of General Relativity (GR), our approach defines the speed of light as c = L_eff / τ, where τ is the universal digital tick (e.g., 0.6 ps) and L_eff is the effective spatial unit. This model not only provides a fundamental explanation for c but also derives the gravitational constant G and addresses dark matter phenomena by attributing observable anomalies to local spatial distortions rather than to changes in time.

Introduction

Traditional GR secures light speed invariance by employing local Lorentz invariance with time dilation and length contraction. However, on a global scale, the coordinate speed of light can vary with the local spacetime environment—an artifact that many have found conceptually unsatisfying. Our digital tick framework posits that time is governed by an unyielding, discrete tick; as such, the observed local effects are due solely to spatial measurement changes.

The Digital Tick Framework

At the core of this model lies the digital tick, an invariant time unit τ that persists regardless of gravitational or inertial effects. Coupled with an effective spatial unit L_eff, it defines the universal speed of light by

c = L_eff / τ

In this picture, an observed time dilation (e.g., the clock “running slow”) is simply a consequence of local spatial stretching. For example, if local space is stretched such that one meter becomes two meters, light takes twice as long to traverse that distance when measured with the expanded ruler. The digital tick itself, however, remains untouched.

Emergent Lorentz Transformations

Despite the discrete underpinning, Lorentz transformations naturally emerge in the continuum limit. In a light-clock experiment:

  • Rest frame: The light pulse travels vertically at speed c with a travel time determined by the fixed mirror separation.
  • Moving observer: The light appears to follow a diagonal path, thus traversing a longer distance and leading to an apparent time dilation. However, this increase in path length is exclusively due to the transformation of spatial measurements—there is no intrinsic change in the digital tick.

In contrast to GR’s coordinate-based adjustments, our method asserts that the invariant speed of light arises strictly from the immutable ratio L_eff / τ, with local deviations attributable to spatial stretching or contraction (and their compensatory effects, such as contraction due to acceleration).

Derivation of Gravitational Constant (G) and Resolution of Dark Matter Effects

An elegant byproduct of this framework is that the gravitational constant, G, emerges directly from the foundational relationship between the invariant tick and the spatial unit—not as an arbitrary scale but as a natural consequence of the digital structure of spacetime.

Furthermore, phenomena typically ascribed to dark matter—such as anomalous galactic rotation and gravitational lensing—can be reinterpreted as the result of local spatial dilations. In our framework, these effects are not due to unseen mass but are instead measurement artifacts from spatial stretching or contraction, all while preserving a globally constant c.

Discussion and Comparative Analysis

Unlike GR, which relies on a complicated interplay of time dilation and length contraction to enforce light speed constancy locally (and incurs a variable global coordinate speed), the digital tick model maintains a universal, unchanging temporal cadence. This simple yet profound distinction allows us to:

  • Eliminate the messy, coordinate-dependent time dilation effects—what appear as changes in clock rates are merely consequences of spatial measurement variations.
  • Derive fundamental constants (such as c and G) from first principles without resorting to ad hoc adjustments.
  • Offer a conceptually transparent resolution to the dark matter problem by attributing gravitational anomalies to spatial distortions, with no need for additional unseen matter.

Conclusions and Future Directions

The digital tick framework presents a compelling reinterpretation of Lorentz transformations by rooting the constancy of the speed of light in an immutable temporal tick and an associated effective spatial unit. This approach not only simplifies our understanding of relativistic phenomena but also provides natural derivations for fundamental constants and resolutions for observed gravitational anomalies.

Future work will focus on the rigorous mathematical derivation of emergent Lorentz transformations from the digital substrate, a detailed exploration of the implications for G and dark matter, and the development of testable predictions to discern this framework from traditional GR.
