Digital-Tick Model: Vacuum Magnetic Field Comparison

--------------------------------------------------------------

This document presents a stress test of the digital-tick model by calculating the vacuum magnetic field

(B_rms) without using divergent integrals or arbitrary renormalization---relying solely on the fundamental digital-tick parameters.

Fundamental Digital-Tick Parameters:

- Tick Time (Δt): 0.6 ps

- Tick Frequency (f₀): \~1.667 THz (f₀ = 1/Δt)

- Characteristic Length (L): L = c/f₀ ≈ 0.18 mm (with c ≈ 3×10\^8 m/s)

- Energy per Tick (E): E = h·f₀ ≈ 6.9 meV (with h ≈ 6.626×10⁻³⁴ Js)

- Effective Fractal Dimension (Dₑff): 2.667

Finite Vacuum Energy Density:

The digital-tick model yields a renormalization-free vacuum energy density:

ρ_vac = (1/2) · ħ·f₀ · [S_Dₑff/(2π)\^(Dₑff)] · [(2π·f₀)/c]\^(Dₑff)

where:

- ħ is the reduced Planck constant (h/(2π))

- S_Dₑff = 2π\^(Dₑff/2) / Γ(Dₑff/2) is the effective unit \"surface area\"

Using the parameters above, a numerical evaluation gives:

ρ_vac ≈ 8.80e-12 J/m³

Vacuum Magnetic Field Estimate:

Assuming that the electromagnetic vacuum energy is partitioned equally between electric and magnetic fields, the magnetic field energy density is defined by:

u_B = B²/(2μ₀)

Equating u_B ≈ ρ_vac leads to an estimate for the rms vacuum magnetic field:

B_rms = √(μ₀ · ρ_vac)

with μ₀ = 4π×10⁻⁷ H/m. The calculation yields:

B_rms ≈ 3.33e-09 T

Comparison with Conventional Expectations:

Standard QED treatments---when regulated with a physical cutoff---predict a vacuum magnetic field amplitude on

the order of a few nanotesla (1 nT = 1×10⁻⁹ T). Our digital-tick model produces a value in the same ballpark, confirming that by imposing an invariant tick, the divergences are eliminated and a finite, physically meaningful result is obtained.

Summary:

- Digital-tick parameters yield a finite vacuum energy density, ρ_vac, without ad hoc renormalization.

This in turn gives a finite vacuum magnetic field:

B_rms ≈ 3.33e-09 T

- The magnitude of the vacuum magnetic field (∼a few ×10⁻⁹ to 10⁻⁸ T) is comparable with conventional (cutoff-regulated) estimates, reinforcing the validity of the digital-tick approach.
