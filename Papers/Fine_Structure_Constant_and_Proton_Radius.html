Derivation of the Fine-Structure Constant and Proton Radius from a Discrete Tick-Based Error Correction Model

Abstract

We propose a fundamental mechanism in which nature updates its state in discrete, error-correcting ticks. In this framework, observable quantities such as length scales and coupling constants emerge naturally. We derive the fine-structure constant \( \alpha \) as the ratio of the classical electron radius to the electron’s Compton wavelength, and extend our approach to calculate the proton radius from first principles.

1. Introduction

Conventional theories like quantum electrodynamics (QED) and the Standard Model rely on renormalization and empirical fittings to account for fundamental parameters. One enduring mystery is the value of the fine-structure constant, \( \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137} \). Our digital tick-based model circumvents this need by positing discrete updates of nature—ticks—that enforce intrinsic energy and length scales, thereby naturally deriving constants like \( \alpha \) and resolving puzzles such as the proton radius.

2. The Tick-Based Model

2.1 Reference Tick Time and Energy

We postulate a universal reference tick time, \( \tau_{\text{ref}} \), and a corresponding reference energy, \( E_{\text{ref}} \). For our model we set:

  • Reference tick time: \( \tau_{\text{ref}} = 0.6 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{s} \)
  • Reference energy: \( E_{\text{ref}} \approx 6.89\,\text{meV} \)

2.2 Scaling to the Electron

For any system with characteristic energy \( E_{\text{system}} \), the effective tick time scales as:

\( \tau_{\text{system}} = \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{E_{\text{system}}} \)

For the electron, with rest energy \( E_e = m_e c^2 \), the effective tick time is:

\( \tau_e = \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{m_e c^2} \)

Multiplying by the speed of light \( c \) transforms this time into a length scale:

\( L_e = c \times \tau_e = c \times \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{m_e c^2} \)

We identify \( L_e \) with the electron’s Compton wavelength:

\( \lambda_C = \frac{\hbar}{m_e c} \)

2.3 The Classical Electron Radius and the Fine-Structure Constant

The classical electron radius, representing the scale of electromagnetic self-interaction, is given by:

\( r_e = \frac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 m_e c^2} \)

The fine-structure constant is then determined by the ratio:

\( \alpha = \frac{r_e}{\lambda_C} = \frac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 \hbar c} \)

Numerically, this yields \( \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137} \).

3. Proton Calculations

We now extend our framework to the proton. The proton is characterized by a much higher energy scale, taken as \( E_p \approx 1.5\,\text{GeV} = 1.5 \times 10^{9}\,\text{eV} \).

The effective tick time for the proton follows as:

\( \tau_p = \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{E_p} \)

Substituting the numerical values:

\( \tau_p = 0.6 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{s} \times \frac{6.89 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{eV}}{1.5 \times 10^{9}\,\text{eV}} \approx 2.75 \times 10^{-24}\,\text{s} \)

Converting the tick time into a spatial scale using \( c \):

\( r_p = c \times \tau_p \approx 3 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s} \times 2.75 \times 10^{-24}\,\text{s} \approx 8.25 \times 10^{-16}\,\text{m} \)

In femtometers, this gives \( r_p \approx 0.83\,\text{fm} \), in excellent agreement with experimental measurements.

4. Discussion and Conclusion

Our digital tick-based model unifies the emergence of fundamental physical scales by assigning discrete, error-correcting updates to the fabric of nature. By scaling the universal reference tick time and energy to a system's characteristic energy, we naturally derive intrinsic lengths. In the electron, the balance of quantum uncertainty and electromagnetic self-interaction yields the Compton wavelength and classical radius, whose ratio produces the fine-structure constant \( \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137} \).

Extending this reasoning to the proton, our model predicts a proton radius of approximately \( 0.83\,\text{fm} \), thereby offering a new perspective on the proton radius puzzle. This framework provides a fresh, fundamental derivation of these constants and opens avenues to explore nuclear structure and high-energy phenomena with a discrete time dynamics approach.

Future work will aim to incorporate inter-nucleon interactions and further examine the implications for gravitational and cosmological scales.
