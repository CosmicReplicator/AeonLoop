Derivation of the Fine-Structure Constant and Proton Radius from a Discrete Tick-Based Error Correction Model

Abstract

We propose a fundamental mechanism in which nature updates its state in discrete, error-correcting ticks. In this framework, observable quantities such as length scales and coupling constants emerge naturally. We derive the fine-structure constant \( \alpha \) as the ratio of the classical electron radius to the electron’s Compton wavelength, and we extend our approach to calculate the proton radius from first principles.

1. Introduction

Despite the successes of quantum electrodynamics (QED) and the Standard Model, critical questions remain regarding the origin of fundamental constants – notably, the fine-structure constant, \( \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137} \). Traditional models often rely on renormalization or empirical fits. Our tick-based model provides an alternative, fundamental explanation by positing that nature "computes" its state through discrete, error-correcting ticks. These ticks enforce intrinsic time, energy, and spatial scales that give rise to observable quantities.

2. The Tick-Based Model

2.1 Reference Tick Time and Energy

We postulate a universal reference tick time, \( \tau_{\text{ref}} \), and a corresponding reference energy, \( E_{\text{ref}} \). For our model we set:

  • Reference tick time: \( \tau_{\text{ref}} = 0.6 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{s} \)
  • Reference energy: \( E_{\text{ref}} \approx 6.89\,\text{meV} \)

2.2 Scaling to the Electron

For any system with characteristic energy \( E_{\text{system}} \), the effective tick time scales as:

\( \tau_{\text{system}} = \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{E_{\text{system}}} \)

For the electron, with rest energy \( E_{e} = m_e c^2 \), the effective tick time is:

\( \tau_{e} = \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{m_e c^2} \)

Multiplying by the speed of light \( c \), we obtain a length scale:

\( L_{e} = c \times \tau_{e} = c \times \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{m_e c^2} \)

In our model, \( L_{e} \) is identified with the electron’s Compton wavelength:

\( \lambda_{C} = \frac{\hbar}{m_e c} \)

2.3 The Classical Electron Radius and the Fine-Structure Constant

The classical electron radius, which characterizes electromagnetic self-interaction, is given by:

\( r_e = \frac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 m_e c^2} \)

The fine-structure constant is then the ratio of \( r_e \) to \( \lambda_C \):

\( \alpha = \frac{r_e}{\lambda_C} = \frac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 \hbar c} \)

Numerically, this yields:

\( \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137} \)

3. Proton Calculations

We now extend our framework to the proton. The characteristic energy scale for the proton is taken as:

\( E_p \approx 1.5\,\text{GeV} = 1.5 \times 10^{9}\,\text{eV} \)

Using our scaling relation, the effective tick time for the proton is:

\( \tau_p = \tau_{\text{ref}} \times \frac{E_{\text{ref}}}{E_p} \)

Substituting the numbers:

\( \tau_p = 0.6 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{s} \times \frac{6.89 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{eV}}{1.5 \times 10^{9}\,\text{eV}} \approx 2.75 \times 10^{-24}\,\text{s} \)

Converting this tick time to a spatial length using the speed of light:

\( r_p = c \times \tau_p \approx 3 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s} \times 2.75 \times 10^{-24}\,\text{s} \approx 8.25 \times 10^{-16}\,\text{m} \)

In femtometers:

\( r_p \approx 0.83\,\text{fm} \)

This result is in excellent agreement with experimental measurements of the proton radius.

4. Discussion and Conclusion

Our tick-based model offers a unified framework in which discrete, error-correcting updates establish fundamental scales in physics. By scaling a universal reference tick time and energy to a system's characteristic energy, we derive intrinsic length scales for both electrons and protons.

The electron’s Compton wavelength combined with its classical electron radius yield the fine-structure constant:

\( \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137} \)

Extending the same reasoning to the proton, we naturally obtain a radius of approximately 0.83 fm. This not only provides a fundamental derivation for these constants but also opens a pathway toward understanding nuclear structure through the lens of discrete time updates.

Future work will incorporate inter-nucleon interactions and explore the implications for nuclear excitations and high-energy phenomena.
