1. Discrete Update of the Electromagnetic Potential

We assume that at each digital tick – every \( \Delta t = 0.6\,\text{ps} \) – the electromagnetic four‑potential, denoted by \( A_\mu(t, x) \), is updated by a rule of the form:

\( A_\mu(t + \Delta t, x) = A_\mu(t, x) + \Delta t \cdot \dot{A_\mu}(t, x) + O((\Delta t)^2) \)

where \( \dot{A_\mu}(t, x) \) is determined by the dynamics (ultimately, by an underlying error‑correction rule). This is analogous to a finite difference approximation:

\( \dot{A_\mu}(t, x) \approx \frac{A_\mu(t + \Delta t, x) - A_\mu(t, x)}{\Delta t} \)

2. Constructing the Field Tensor from Finite Differences

Recall that the electromagnetic field tensor is defined through the potential as:

\( F_{\mu \nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu \)

The partial derivatives are approximated by finite differences. For example, the time derivative is given by:

\( \partial_t A_\nu(t, x) \approx \frac{A_\nu(t + \Delta t, x) - A_\nu(t, x)}{\Delta t} \)

Similarly, spatial derivatives are replaced by finite differences over the appropriate spatial intervals. In the limit as \( \Delta t \) (and the spatial step \( \Delta x \)) \( \to 0 \), these finite differences approach the standard derivatives, recovering the familiar definition of \( F_{\mu \nu} \).

3. Discrete Version of the Lagrangian and Euler--Lagrange Equations

For electromagnetism, the classical Lagrangian density is given by:

\( L = -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu \nu} F^{\mu \nu} \)

We formulate a lattice version of this Lagrangian by replacing the derivatives with finite differences. Defining an action by summing over the nodes and digital ticks (e.g. \( t,\, t + \Delta t,\, t + 2\Delta t, \dots \)), the discrete Euler--Lagrange equations take the form:

\( \frac{A_\mu(t + \Delta t, x) - A_\mu(t, x)}{\Delta t} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial (\partial_t A^\mu)} \)

and in the continuum limit (\( \Delta t \to 0 \)), we recover the standard Euler--Lagrange equations. Variation of the continuum action then yields:

\( \partial_\mu F^{\mu \nu} = J^\nu \)

which are precisely Maxwell's equations (with source terms, where \( J^\nu \) is the four‑current).

4. Emergence of Maxwell's Equations from the Discrete Model

Thus, by:

  • \( (i) \) updating \( A_\mu(t, x) \) via our universal digital tick,
  • \( (ii) \) computing the field tensor \( F_{\mu \nu} \) as the finite differences of \( A_\mu \), and
  • \( (iii) \) formulating a discrete version of the electromagnetic Lagrangian and applying the Euler--Lagrange procedure,

we obtain, in the continuum limit, the familiar differential equations:

\( \nabla \cdot E = \rho \)
\( \nabla \times B - \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} = J \)
\( \nabla \cdot B = 0 \)
\( \nabla \times E + \frac{\partial B}{\partial t} = 0 \)

where the electric field \( E \) and the magnetic field \( B \) are identified from the components of \( F_{\mu \nu} \). The digital model's error‑correction at each tick prevents divergences from emerging, preempting the need for renormalization and yielding a deterministic evolution that aligns with Maxwell's equations.

5. Summary

The discrete update with \( \Delta t = 0.6\,\text{ps} \) replaces continuous derivatives with finite differences.

The electromagnetic field tensor is computed via finite differences of \( A_\mu \).

A discrete Lagrangian formulation and the Euler--Lagrange procedure yield Maxwell's equations in the continuum limit.

Gauge invariance is maintained by ensuring that the update rules respect the transformation \( A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu \Lambda \).

The inherent digital error‑correction mechanism naturally eliminates divergences and the need for renormalization, leading to a deterministic model.
