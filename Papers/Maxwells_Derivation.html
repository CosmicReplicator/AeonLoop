Electromagnetism from Digital Ticks: A Discrete Approach to Maxwell’s Equations

Abstract

In this paper, we derive the familiar equations of electromagnetism from first principles using the Digital‑Tick (AeonLoop) framework. In our approach, time is not continuous but consists of discrete, invariant ticks (with a tick duration of \( \Delta t = 0.6\,\text{ps} \)). By replacing conventional derivatives with finite differences and incorporating an error‑correction mechanism at each tick, we reconstruct the electromagnetic four‑potential, field tensor, and ultimately the full set of Maxwell’s equations. This discrete formulation not only reproduces the standard results in the continuum limit but also naturally avoids divergences and the need for renormalization, thereby offering a unified, deterministic perspective on electromagnetism.

1. Discrete Update of the Electromagnetic Potential

At the heart of our approach is the idea that the electromagnetic four‑potential \( A_\mu(t, x) \) is updated only at discrete tick instants. No evolution is seen in between. We assume that at each digital tick the potential updates according to:

\( A_\mu(t + \Delta t, x) = A_\mu(t, x) + \Delta t \cdot \dot{A_\mu}(t, x) + O((\Delta t)^2) \)

Here, the time derivative is approximated by the finite difference,

\( \dot{A_\mu}(t, x) \approx \frac{A_\mu(t + \Delta t, x) - A_\mu(t, x)}{\Delta t}. \)

This discrete update is analogous to standard finite difference methods used in numerical analysis; however, in our model it is fundamental, governing the evolution of fields at every tick.

2. Constructing the Field Tensor from Finite Differences

The electromagnetic field tensor is defined by the derivatives of the potential. In our discrete setting, we replace the usual derivatives with finite differences. For instance, the time derivative for a component is written as:

\( \partial_t A_\nu(t, x) \approx \frac{A_\nu(t + \Delta t, x) - A_\nu(t, x)}{\Delta t}. \)

Similarly, spatial derivatives are approximated using the discrete spatial step \( \Delta x \). Thus, the field tensor

\( F_{\mu \nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu \)

is calculated entirely from finite differences. In the limit as \( \Delta t,\Delta x \to 0 \) the standard, continuous form of \( F_{\mu \nu} \) is recovered.

3. Discrete Lagrangian and Euler–Lagrange Equations

The classical Lagrangian density for electromagnetism is given by:

\( L = -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu \nu} F^{\mu \nu}. \)

In our digital‑tick picture, we formulate a lattice version of this Lagrangian. Time is discretized into ticks, and the action is defined by summing the Lagrangian over all nodes. The discrete Euler–Lagrange equations take a form analogous to their continuum counterpart:

\( \frac{A_\mu(t + \Delta t, x) - A_\mu(t, x)}{\Delta t} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial (\partial_t A^\mu)}. \)

As we take the continuum limit \( \Delta t \to 0 \), these finite difference equations reproduce the usual Euler–Lagrange equations. Variation of the action leads directly to Maxwell’s equations:

\( \partial_\mu F^{\mu \nu} = J^\nu, \)

where \( J^\nu \) is the four‑current.

4. Emergence of Maxwell's Equations from the Discrete Model

By combining:

  • \( (i) \) the digital tick update of \( A_\mu(t, x) \),
  • \( (ii) \) the finite difference construction of \( F_{\mu \nu} \), and
  • \( (iii) \) the discrete Lagrangian formulation and Euler–Lagrange equations,

we find that in the continuum limit our model reproduces the familiar set of Maxwell's equations:

\( \nabla \cdot E = \rho, \)
\( \nabla \times B - \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} = J, \)
\( \nabla \cdot B = 0, \)
\( \nabla \times E + \frac{\partial B}{\partial t} = 0. \)

Importantly, our digital update is designed to incorporate an inherent error‑correction. This mechanism prevents the emergence of divergences, preempting the need for renormalization. Moreover, the update rules are constructed so that gauge invariance remains intact—under the transformation \[ A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu \Lambda, \] the discrete formulation is unchanged.

5. Summary

In summary, the digital‑tick framework allows us to:

  • Replace continuous derivatives with finite differences based on the invariant tick interval (\( \Delta t = 0.6\,\text{ps} \)).
  • Construct the electromagnetic field tensor \( F_{\mu \nu} \) via these finite differences.
  • Formulate a discrete version of the electromagnetic Lagrangian and, via the Euler–Lagrange procedure, recover Maxwell's equations in the continuum limit.
  • Maintain gauge invariance and eliminate divergences through a built-in error‑correction mechanism at each tick.

This deterministic, discrete model not only reproduces the standard electromagnetic theory when \( \Delta t \) (and \( \Delta x \)) tend to zero, but it also suggests that at the most fundamental level, nature may operate via digital updates rather than a smooth continuum.
