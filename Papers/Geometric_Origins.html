These derivations encourage a perspective in which seemingly arbitrary constants are in fact emergent from the discrete, geometric fabric of nature. # 1. Introduction The standard view considers many fundamental constants as empirically determined “missing numbers.” For instance, the fine-structure constant, \(\alpha \approx 1/137\), is introduced into quantum electrodynamics without a deeper explanation, much like the reduced Planck constant \[ \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi}\,, \] which emphasizes the role of circular symmetry or full rotations in the quantization of angular momentum. In our Digital‑Tick model the universe is updated in discrete time steps (ticks), with each tick contributing a fixed phase increment. We show that full \(2\pi\) phase closure, together with an effective fractal integration (with dimensionality \(D_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 2.667\)), naturally yields both an energy amplification factor and an effective charge function that reproduce \(\alpha\) from first principles. # 2. The Digital‑Tick Framework and Phase Closure ## 2.1. Discrete Ticks and Base Parameters We assume that time is not continuous but is instead comprised of invariant ticks: - **Tick Duration:** \[ \Delta t = 0.6\,\text{ps}. \] - **Base Frequency:** \[ f_0 = \frac{1}{\Delta t} \approx 1.667\,\text{THz}. \] - **Effective Spatial Scale:** During one tick, light travels \[ L_{\mathrm{eff}} = c\,\Delta t \approx 0.18\,\text{mm}. \] ## 2.2. Discrete Phase Increments Each tick provides a phase increment determined by dividing a full rotation (\(2\pi\)) into \(N=7\) discrete steps: \[ \Delta \varphi = \frac{2\pi}{7}. \] Thus, after 7 ticks the system has (naïvely) accumulated a total phase of \[ 7\,\Delta \varphi = 2\pi. \] ## 2.3. Effective Phase Closure and the "7 Minus 1" Insight A key observation is that the base state already “contains” a full \(2\pi\) (or full circle). Hence, rather than 7 independent contributions, only the additional \(N-1\) ticks are effective. For \(N=7\) ticks, \[ N_{\text{effective}} = 7 - 1 = 6. \] If each effective tick contributes an energy increment proportional to \(\frac{1}{2\alpha}\), then the overall energy amplification factor is \[ A_{\text{eff}} = \frac{6}{2\alpha} = \frac{3}{\alpha}. \] By setting this equal to the observed amplification factor of approximately 411, we obtain: \[ \frac{3}{\alpha} \approx 411 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \alpha \approx \frac{3}{411} \approx 0.0073, \] which is consistent with \(\alpha \approx \frac{1}{137}\). # 3. The Effective Charge Function and Fractal Integration In our framework, the effective electromagnetic coupling is dictated by the geometry of space. We postulate an effective charge function: \[ F(D) \sim \exp\Big[-\beta\,(D-2)\Big], \] where: - \(D\) is the effective spatial dimension, - \(\beta\) is a parameter arising from the dynamics of the discrete update. For our model, the effective fractal dimension is \[ D_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 2.667, \] so that \[ D_{\mathrm{eff}} - 2 \approx 0.667. \] Taking \(\beta \approx 7.38\) (which emerges from matching the discrete energy scaling), we have: \[ F(D_{\mathrm{eff}}) \sim \exp\Big[-7.38 \times 0.667\Big]. \] Evaluating the product gives: \[ 7.38 \times 0.667 \approx 4.923, \] and hence, \[ F(D_{\mathrm{eff}}) \sim \exp\Big[-4.923\Big] \approx 0.0073. \] Thus we can equate the effective charge function to the fine-structure constant: \[ \alpha \sim \exp\Big[-\beta\,(D_{\mathrm{eff}}-2)\Big] \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \alpha \approx \frac{1}{137}. \] # 4.
