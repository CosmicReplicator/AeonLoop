Introduction

In our modified framework, cosmic evolution is governed by a discrete ticking mechanism, where each tick (with duration \( \tau = 0.6\,\text{ps} \)) enforces an error‐correction on the coherent energy. Combining this with an effective fractal spatial integration in \( D_{\text{eff}} = 2.667 \) dimensions, we recast the Friedmann equations to describe a universe driven solely by baryonic matter. Here, the familiar expansion is reinterpreted as the cumulative effect of tick‐induced phase drift.

Modified Friedmann Equation

The standard Friedmann equation is given by: \[ \left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{k}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda}{3}. \] In our discrete scheme, we replace the continuous derivative with a finite difference, so that \[ \frac{\dot{a}}{a} \approx \frac{a_{N+1} - a_N}{\tau\, a_N}, \] yielding: \[ \left(\frac{a_{N+1} - a_N}{\tau\, a_N}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G_{\text{eff}}}{3}\rho_{\text{baryon}} - \frac{k_{\text{eff}}}{a_N^2}. \] Here, since our first-principles derivation shows that all energy is carried by baryons (\( p_{\text{total}} = p_{\text{matter}} \)), we set \(\Lambda \approx 0\). Also, the 3D integration is modified to: \[ dV_{\text{eff}} \propto r^{D_{\text{eff}}-1}\, dr = r^{1.667}\, dr, \] so that the gravitational constant is recalibrated as: \[ G_{\text{eff}} = G \left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{3-D_{\text{eff}}} = G \left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{0.333}, \] where \(r_0\) is a suitable normalization scale.

Recalculating Cosmic Evolution

For a spatially flat universe (i.e. we set \( k_{\text{eff}} = 0 \)) and assuming that the baryonic matter density scales as \(\rho_{\text{baryon}}(a) = \rho_{b0}(a_0/a)^3\), the discrete evolution of the cosmic scale factor is determined by: \[ a_{N+1} = a_N + \tau\, a_N \, H_N, \] where the discrete “Hubble parameter” is: \[ H_N = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G_{\text{eff}}}{3}\rho_{b0}\left(\frac{a_0}{a_N}\right)^3 }. \] In the continuous limit this reproduces the conventional \( a(t) \propto t^{2/3} \) behavior (as expected for a matter-dominated universe).

As an example, consider present-day conditions with the normalization \(a_0 = 1\) and take an estimated baryonic density of \(\rho_{b0} \sim 3 \times 10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}\). Assuming a nominal value \(G_{\text{eff}} \approx G\) (if the normalization \(r/r_0\) is chosen appropriately), we obtain: \[ H_0 = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho_{b0}} \approx 1.3\times10^{-18}\,\text{s}^{-1}. \] In our scheme, cosmic time is given by \[ t = N\,\tau. \] For an age of the universe \(t \sim 4.35\times10^{17}\,\text{s}\) (about 13.8 billion years), the total tick count is \(N \approx 7.25\times10^{29}\). Iterating the discrete evolution equation over this enormous number of ticks recovers the expected expansion history, with the scale factor increasing as a function of \(N\) (or \(t\)) in a way that mirrors the \(t^{2/3}\) scaling.

Tick-Induced Redshift

In our picture, the conventional redshift relationship: \[ 1+z = \frac{a(t_0)}{a(t_{\text{emit}})} \] is reinterpreted. Each tick imparts a small phase shift \(\delta\), and the cumulative redshift is given by: \[ 1+z \approx \prod_{i=1}^{N}(1+\delta_i) \approx \exp\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N}\delta_i\right). \] If the phase shift per tick is nearly constant, i.e. \(\delta_i \approx \delta\), then for \(N\) ticks: \[ z \approx N \,\delta. \] This establishes a direct link between the discrete ticking dynamics and observable redshift.

Discussion

The recalculation of the cosmos using our modified Friedmann equations highlights that, when time is discretized and space is treated through a fractal lens, cosmic expansion emerges naturally from the intrinsic dynamics of baryonic matter. No additional energy (or dark energy) is required—the error-correction mechanism built into every tick maintains energy balance, and the cumulative phase drift yields the redshift observed in distant galaxies.

While a full numerical simulation would involve iterating the recurrence relation \( a_{N+1} = a_N + \tau\, a_N\, H_N \) over \( \sim 10^{29} \) ticks, our analytic estimates show that the discrete ticking framework is consistent with a matter-dominated expansion history. Moreover, by linking the tick-induced phase shifts to redshift, we establish a novel, testable connection between cosmic evolution and the fundamental granularity of time.

Conclusion

Our recalcitration of cosmic evolution using the modified discrete Friedmann equations demonstrates that a universe governed solely by recalibrated baryonic matter—and evolving via digital ticks—can reproduce the observed expansion history without invoking extraneous energy components. Future work will involve detailed numerical simulations and a confrontation of these predictions with observational data to further validate the discrete tick model.
