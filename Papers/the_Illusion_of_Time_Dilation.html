Introduction" content: | The conventional interpretation of time dilation suggests that clocks in high-speed motion or intense gravitational fields tick slower than those in inertial or lower gravitational settings. This idea has been bolstered by countless experiments and observations. However, these interpretations often assume measurement devices—specifically the spatial instruments that underpin our definition of time—remain invariant across different environments. Pulsars, rotating neutron stars with phenomenal clock-like precision, provide a natural laboratory for investigating time’s behavior in diverse gravitational conditions. Their ticks have been observed to be remarkably consistent, regardless of the surrounding density or gravitational potential. In our discrete-tick framework, we postulate that time is governed by a fixed digital tick of 0.6 ps, which, through the relation \[ \Delta x = c\,\Delta t, \] yields a nominal spatial unit of 0.18 mm. In this view, what appears as time dilation in standard analyses arises from neglecting the fact that the spatial “ruler” dynamically recalibrates with environmental changes. This paper presents the theoretical basis for our approach and examines pulsar measurements as definitive proof that time, as measured by our digital tick, remains invariant. - title: "2.1. In our framework, while the digital tick remains a constant 0.6 ps, any environmental variation is absorbed by recalibrating the spatial metric. Failure to account for this dynamic recalibration results in a misinterpretation: if the ruler is assumed invariant, any discrepancy appears as if time itself is dilating. Proper spatial adjustment preserves the constancy of the tick, debunking the concept of intrinsic time dilation. - title: "3.1.
